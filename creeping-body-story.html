The Creeping Body

A 50-page publication that maps how the Supreme Court crept into an unchecked policy-making body — making dense constitutional law tangible through editorial design and visual timelines.

Designer — Jack Murray·2024·50-page publication, editorial design·Author, designer, timeline graphics, typesetting

The Brief

Legal cases are among the most impactful and least understood pieces of political action. By mapping and compartmentalizing dense essays, the publication makes the Court’s quiet power legible — letting more readers grasp the value, and the danger, these bodies hold.

Concept

Four interlocking essays — campaign finance, “history and tradition,” the Court as a political body, and a full analysis of United States v. Rahimi — argue a single thesis: a branch that defines its own jurisdiction and reshapes rights without oversight.

Process · 01

Type chosen for legibility

Helvetica was studied across its full weight range, then paired with Lato — thinner, modern forms to cut through the stuffy density of legal language.

Process · 02

Information design as model

The visual language borrowed from the NYC Subway Map and Polling Canada — clarity systems that turn complexity into something a reader can follow at a glance.

Process · 03

A grid built for breathing room

A custom 8.5″ × 9.5″ page on a five-column, three-row grid gives dense argument the open space and legibility a standard format would deny it.

Select Spreads

Colored timelines map legal history across eras — each chapter opening on a full-bleed spread that orients the reader before the argument begins.
